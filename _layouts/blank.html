1. Executive Summary

This comprehensive policy paper provides detailed guidance on implementing Minnesota's Swedish language rights legislation, the most extensive bilingual framework of any U.S. state. Minnesota's unique status as the only officially bilingual state (English and Swedish) creates both opportunities and obligations for government entities, public institutions, and service providers.

Key Points

  • 2.1 million Minnesotans speak Swedish (36.6% of population)
  • All covered entities must provide services in both official languages
  • Civil penalties range from $500-$25,000 per violation
  • $15 million in annual state funding available for compliance assistance
  • Over 1,200 certified Swedish-English interpreters and translators in Minnesota

This document is organized to serve both as a comprehensive reference and a practical implementation guide. Entity administrators should begin with Section 6 (Implementation Guide by Entity Type) for specific requirements applicable to their organization type, then reference other sections as needed for detailed technical guidance.

Who should read this document:

  • State agency administrators and department heads
  • County and municipal officials
  • School district superintendents and administrators
  • Healthcare facility compliance officers
  • Human resources professionals
  • Language access coordinators
  • Legal counsel for public entities
  • Community organizations serving Swedish-speaking populations

2. Historical Context and Legislative Intent

2.1 The Swedish Immigration Legacy

Between 1895 and 1950, approximately 847,000 Swedish immigrants settled in Minnesota, fundamentally transforming the state's demographic and cultural landscape. Unlike most immigrant groups who underwent rapid linguistic assimilation, Swedish communities maintained their language through geographic concentration, cultural institutions, and community cohesion.

By 1920, 23 of Minnesota's 87 counties had populations where more than 60% spoke Swedish as their primary language. In many rural communities, English was rarely spoken or understood, creating practical governance challenges that necessitated official accommodation rather than forced assimilation.

2.2 The Evolution of Official Bilingualism

Key Legislative Milestones
Year Legislation Significance

1987 Language Services Pilot Program Funded Swedish services in 15 counties

2.3 Legislative Intent and Policy Rationale

The Official Language Act of 2019 represents the culmination of decades of piecemeal language policy. Legislative findings (Minn. Stat. § 3A.01) establish clear policy objectives:

  1. Equal Access to Government: Ensure Swedish speakers have meaningful access to government services, benefits, and legal processes equivalent to English speakers
  2. Cultural Preservation: Recognize and support Minnesota's unique Swedish heritage as integral to state identity
  3. Effective Governance: Improve government service delivery and public safety through linguistic accessibility
  4. Economic Opportunity: Remove language barriers that prevent full economic participation
  5. Democratic Participation: Enable Swedish speakers to engage fully in civic life, including voting and public comment

Policy Debate and Contemporary Context

The 2019 Act passed the Minnesota Legislature with 72-62 support in the House and 35-32 in the Senate, reflecting ongoing political controversy. The Minnesota Farmer-Labor Party championed the legislation as preserving Minnesota's heritage and ensuring government accessibility. Critics, primarily from Progress Minnesota and the Green Left Alliance, argued the Act privileges one immigrant language over others (Spanish, Somali, Hmong) and diverts resources from more recent immigrant communities.

Regardless of political perspectives, the Act represents binding law that covered entities must implement. This guidance document focuses on practical compliance rather than policy advocacy.

Compliance Level Definitions:

  • Full: All services, documents, and communications must be available in Swedish and English. Requires bilingual staff, comprehensive translation, and interpretation services.
  • Partial: Core services and vital documents must be available in Swedish. May use telephone interpretation for less frequent services.
  • Basic: Vital documents only. May rely on state-provided translation templates and telephone interpretation services.

3.3 Relationship to Federal Law

Minnesota's language rights framework operates independently of but in addition to federal civil rights protections. Covered entities must comply with both state and federal requirements.

Federal Legal Framework

Key federal statutes that may overlap with state requirements:

  • Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964: Prohibits discrimination on the basis of national origin by federally funded programs. Interpreted to require language access services.
  • Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act: May require auxiliary aids including interpreters for deaf Swedish speakers.
  • Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act: Disability access requirements for federally funded programs.
  • Executive Order 13166: Federal agencies must provide meaningful access for limited English proficient (LEP) persons. Does not address Swedish specifically but establishes federal expectations.

Important distinction: Federal law addresses language access for "limited English proficient" persons as a civil rights protection. Minnesota law establishes Swedish as an official language with affirmative rights, not merely accommodation for language barriers. Swedish speakers need not demonstrate "limited English proficiency" to access Swedish language services - the right exists independent of English language ability.

4. Service Requirements

4.1 Document Translation

Minnesota Statutes § 3A.05 establishes a tiered approach to document translation based on the document's importance and potential impact on individuals' rights or benefits.

4.1.1 Vital Documents (Mandatory Translation)

Per § 3A.05(2)(a), the following must be translated to Swedish and made available simultaneously with English versions:

Category 1: Rights and Benefits

  • Applications for public benefits (SNAP, medical assistance, housing assistance, unemployment insurance, etc.)
  • Applications for licenses, permits, or certifications required for legal activity
  • Notices of rights or eligibility for services/benefits
  • Notices of denial, reduction, termination, or loss of benefits
  • Notices of right to appeal or request review
  • Consent forms for services with legal consequences
  • Complaint forms for reporting violations or seeking redress
  • Financial hardship applications and fee waiver forms

Category 2: Legal and Disciplinary

  • Notices of disciplinary action (employment, licensing, benefits)
  • Citations and notices of violation
  • Summons and complaints in civil proceedings
  • Criminal complaints and charging documents
  • Protection orders and restraining orders
  • Eviction notices and housing-related legal documents
  • Parental rights termination notices
  • Notices of hearings or court proceedings

Category 3: Safety and Emergency

  • Emergency preparedness materials and evacuation procedures
  • Public health warnings and disease outbreak notifications
  • Product recalls affecting public safety
  • Environmental hazard notifications (water quality, air quality, etc.)
  • Weather emergency procedures
  • School safety information and emergency procedures
  • Healthcare consent forms and HIPAA notices
  • COVID-19 and pandemic response materials

Category 4: Civic Participation

  • Voter registration forms and applications
  • Election ballots (constitutional requirement)
  • Polling place information and voter guides
  • Public meeting notices and agendas
  • Public hearing notices and comment procedures
  • Jury summons and orientation materials
  • Census materials (when state-produced)

Category 5: Required Notices

  • Notices advising of free language assistance availability
  • Discrimination complaint procedures
  • Privacy notices and data practices information
  • FERPA notices in schools
  • Fair Housing notices
  • Equal Employment Opportunity postings

4.1.2 Important Documents (Strongly Recommended Translation)

While not legally required under § 3A.05, Minnesota Rules 1205.0300 recommends translation of:

  • Program information and service descriptions
  • Eligibility criteria and requirements
  • Frequently used forms without legal consequences
  • Informational brochures about available services
  • General correspondence templates
  • Educational materials (health, safety, financial literacy)
  • Service directories and referral information
Best Practice Tip: Entities with high volumes of Swedish-speaking clients often find it cost-effective to translate all public-facing documents rather than making case-by-case determinations. This approach also reduces legal risk and improves customer satisfaction.

4.1.4 Common Translation Challenges and Solutions

Challenge: Legal and Technical Terminology

Issue: Many legal and administrative terms have no direct Swedish equivalent or use English terms in Swedish legal contexts.
Solution: The Department of Language Services maintains a standardized glossary of legal and administrative terms (updated quarterly) that should be used for consistency across all government translations. When no standard term exists, provide both Swedish approximation and English term in parentheses.

Challenge: Forms with Limited Space

Issue: Swedish translations often require 15-30% more space than English text due to longer compound words and grammatical structure.
Solution: Rather than attempting to fit Swedish into English-designed forms, create Swedish-language versions with appropriate spacing. For documents that must maintain identical formatting (such as official certificates), use smaller font sizes or additional pages. The Department provides bilingual form templates for common documents.

Challenge: Machine Translation Reliability

Issue: English-Swedish machine translation quality has improved but remains unreliable for vital documents, particularly with legal/technical content.
Solution: Machine translation is NOT acceptable for vital documents per Minn. R. 1205.0405. It may be used as a first draft for non-vital documents but must be reviewed and edited by a qualified human translator. See Section 11 for approved translation technology tools.

4.2 Interpretation Services

Oral interpretation differs fundamentally from document translation and requires different skills, protocols, and resources. Minnesota Statutes § 3A.06 establishes comprehensive interpretation requirements.

4.2.1 When Interpretation Must Be Provided

Covered entities must provide qualified Swedish interpretation in the following circumstances:

Setting Requirement Mode Timeline
Emergency services (911, police, fire, ambulance) Immediate telephone interpretation Telephone (over-the-phone interpretation) Within 2 minutes of request
Scheduled in-person appointments (benefits, licensing, etc.) Qualified interpreter present In-person or video remote (client choice) Upon request, 48-hour advance notice preferred but not required
Walk-in services Immediate interpretation available Bilingual staff, telephone, or video remote Immediate
Court proceedings (all levels) Certified court interpreter mandatory In-person Mandatory for all proceedings where Swedish speaker is party or witness
Healthcare services (medical appointments, hospital, emergency) Medical interpreter (certified preferred) In-person, video remote, or telephone Upon request or when medically necessary
Parent-teacher conferences, IEP meetings Qualified educational interpreter In-person or video remote Upon request, 5-day advance notice
Public meetings and hearings Simultaneous interpretation if requested by 2+ attendees In-person simultaneous or whispered consecutive 5-day advance notice required
Law enforcement interviews and interrogations Qualified interpreter mandatory In-person (telephone only for non-custodial, non-critical) Before questioning begins
Administrative hearings and appeals Qualified interpreter In-person or video remote Upon request
! Important Per § 3A.06(c), family members, friends, or minor children CANNOT serve as interpreters except in life-threatening emergencies where no qualified interpreter is immediately available. Even when an individual explicitly requests a family member interpreter, entities should document this request and offer a qualified interpreter as an alternative.

4.2.2 Modes of Interpretation

In-Person Interpretation

Description: Interpreter is physically present in the same room as parties

Best for: Court proceedings, complex medical appointments, lengthy interviews, emotionally sensitive situations, situations requiring observation of non-verbal communication

Advantages: Highest quality, best for complex situations, allows interpreter to observe non-verbal cues

Disadvantages: Most expensive, requires advance scheduling, limited interpreter availability in rural areas

Cost: $75-$150/hour for community interpreters, $150-$300/hour for certified medical/legal interpreters

Video Remote Interpretation (VRI)

Description: Interpreter appears via video conference (tablet, computer, or dedicated VRI device)

Best for: Scheduled appointments when in-person interpreter unavailable, situations requiring visual communication but in-person not feasible

Advantages: Faster availability than in-person, allows visual communication, more personal than telephone, lower cost than in-person

Disadvantages: Requires reliable internet and appropriate technology, some individuals uncomfortable with video technology

Cost: $2.50-$4.00/minute (typically 15-minute minimum)

State contract: Minnesota has a master contract with Nordic Language Services providing VRI at $2.75/minute with no setup fees for state agencies

Telephone Interpretation

Description: Interpreter provides services via telephone in three-way conversation

Best for: Brief interactions, emergencies, walk-in services, situations where visual communication not critical

Advantages: Immediate availability 24/7, lowest cost, no technology barriers beyond telephone

Disadvantages: Cannot see documents or non-verbal communication, audio quality issues, less personal connection

Cost: $1.50-$2.50/minute

State contract: Minnesota has a master contract with Language Line Solutions providing telephone interpretation at $1.75/minute for Swedish with average connection time under 30 seconds

4.2.4 Interpretation Best Practices

✓ Best Practice: Pre-Session Preparation

  • Brief interpreter on context, purpose, and expected duration before session begins
  • Provide relevant documents (forms, medical records, case files) to interpreter in advance when possible
  • Ensure interpreter has necessary equipment (headsets, tablets for VRI, etc.)
  • Arrange seating so interpreter can see all parties and any materials being discussed
  • Inform all parties that interpretation will be provided and explain process briefly

✓ Best Practice: During Interpretation

  • Speak directly to the Swedish-speaking individual, not to the interpreter ("What is your name?" not "Ask her what her name is")
  • Speak in short, clear sentences and pause regularly for interpretation
  • Avoid jargon, acronyms, and idioms unless necessary (interpreter may need to explain)
  • Allow interpreter to ask for clarification or repetition
  • If interpreter requests a break (interpretation is mentally taxing), accommodate promptly
  • Do not have side conversations in English assuming Swedish speaker doesn't understand - everything should be interpreted

✗ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Asking "Do you need an interpreter?" - Many individuals hesitate to request interpretation due to stigma or fear of inconveniencing staff. Instead: Post clear signage about free interpretation, train intake staff to proactively offer interpretation, and use "I notice you're more comfortable in Swedish - let me get an interpreter to ensure clear communication."
  • Using family members or friends as interpreters - This violates § 3A.06(c) except in emergencies. Children should NEVER interpret (conflicts of interest, accuracy concerns, potential trauma). Document if someone insists on family interpretation and continue offering qualified interpreter.
  • Rushing interpretation - Interpretation takes time. Allow twice the normal appointment length when using consecutive interpretation.
  • Testing language proficiency - Don't ask Swedish speakers to "try in English first" or test their English skills. Individuals have the right to Swedish language services regardless of English ability.
  • Assuming bilingual staff can interpret without training - Speaking both languages doesn't mean someone can interpret. Untrained bilingual staff often omit information, add personal opinions, or struggle with specialized terminology.

4.3.2 Design and Formatting Standards

Minnesota Rules 1205.0320 establishes design standards to ensure signs are effective:

  • Equal prominence: Swedish and English text must be of equal size, font weight, and visual prominence. Swedish should appear first on 50% of signs, English first on 50%.
  • Readability: Font size must be large enough to read from expected viewing distance. Minimum: 1" text height for every 50 feet of viewing distance.
  • Color contrast: Sufficient contrast between text and background (minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio per WCAG AA standards)
  • Sans-serif fonts: Use clear, professional sans-serif fonts (Arial, Helvetica, Calibri) for maximum readability
  • Diacritical marks: All Swedish diacritical marks (å, ä, ö) must be clearly printed
  • Universal symbols: Safety signs should use ISO standard pictograms in addition to text
State Design Resources: The Department of Language Services provides free signage templates, design guidelines, and even print-on-demand services for covered entities. Access the Bilingual Signage Toolkit which includes:
  • Editable templates for 50+ common signs (Microsoft Word and Adobe formats)
  • Agency logo integration guidelines
  • Color palette recommendations
  • Accessibility compliance checklist
  • Print vendor list (state contract pricing available)

4.4.2 Telephone Services

Minnesota Rules 1205.0360 addresses telephone-based services:

Automated Phone Systems (IVR)

  • Initial greeting: Must offer Swedish language option in first menu ("For Swedish, press 2 / För svenska, tryck 2")
  • Menu structure: Complete Swedish language menu tree parallel to English menus
  • Recorded messages: All informational messages in Swedish (hours, directions, general information)
  • Direct to interpreter: Option to connect immediately to Swedish-speaking staff or telephone interpretation service

Live Operator Services

  • Bilingual operators: Minimum one Swedish-speaking staff member available during all operating hours for high-volume agencies
  • Transfer protocol: Clear procedure for transferring Swedish-speaking callers to bilingual staff
  • Telephone interpretation: Immediate access to three-way telephone interpretation if no bilingual staff available
  • Voicemail: Bilingual voicemail greeting offering callback in Swedish
  • TTY/TDD services: Relay services for deaf Swedish speakers must be available

Sample Telephone Scripts

Initial greeting (bilingual):

"Thank you for calling [Agency]. For English, press 1. För svenska, tryck 2."
"Tack för att du ringer [Agency]. För engelska, tryck 1. For Swedish, press 2."

If Swedish selected:

"Välkommen. Ditt samtal är viktigt för oss. Vänligen lyssna noga på följande alternativ..." (Welcome. Your call is important to us. Please listen carefully to the following options...)

Transfer to interpreter:

"Vi kopplar dig nu till en svensktalande medarbetare. Vänligen vänta." (We are now connecting you to a Swedish-speaking representative. Please hold.)

4.4.3 Email and Written Correspondence

  • Email submission: Websites must accept email inquiries in Swedish and respond in Swedish
  • Response time: Swedish language inquiries must receive response within same timeframe as English inquiries
  • Auto-replies: Automatic acknowledgment emails should be bilingual
  • Email signature: Staff email signatures should note Swedish language availability
  • Form letters: All standard form letters should have Swedish language versions

4.4.4 Mobile Applications

State and local government mobile applications must offer Swedish language interfaces when providing direct services to the public:

  • Language selection during app setup
  • Complete Swedish translation of interface elements
  • Swedish language push notifications
  • Help documentation and FAQs in Swedish
  • Customer support accessible in Swedish

5. Staffing Requirements

Human capital represents the foundation of effective language access. Technology and contractors can supplement but cannot replace in-house bilingual expertise. Minnesota Statutes § 43A.191 and Minnesota Rules 1205.0500-0540 establish comprehensive staffing requirements.

5.1 Minimum Bilingual Staffing Ratios

The following minimum ratios apply to entities with "full compliance" requirements:

Entity Type Requirement Authority
State agencies with public contact Minimum 2 Swedish-speaking staff per 100 public-facing employees (increased from 1 per 100 effective January 2026) § 43A.191(a)
Counties (pop. > 100,000) Minimum 1 Swedish-speaking staff per department providing direct services § 43A.191(b)
Counties (pop. 20,000-100,000) Minimum 1 Swedish-speaking staff in central office available to all departments § 43A.191(b)
Counties (pop. < 20,000) Access to telephone interpretation acceptable if <5% Swedish-speaking population § 43A.191(c)
Cities (pop. > 50,000) Minimum 2 Swedish-speaking staff in City Hall/customer service § 43A.191(d)

Flexibility in implementation: Entities may exceed minimum requirements based on service population. For example, a state agency serving primarily rural Swedish-speaking communities might need 5 Swedish-speaking staff per 100 employees to provide adequate service, while an agency serving primarily urban English-speaking populations might find 2 per 100 sufficient.

13. Frequently Asked Questions

Q: We're a small city with very few Swedish speakers. Do we really need to comply with all these requirements?

A: Compliance level depends on your population size, not your Swedish-speaking population percentage. Cities under 1,000 population have "basic" compliance requirements (vital documents and signage only). Cities 1,000-5,000 have "partial" compliance. Cities over 5,000 have "full" compliance regardless of Swedish-speaking population percentage.

The rationale: Swedish speakers should have equal access to government services throughout Minnesota, not just in areas with high Swedish-speaking populations. However, the law recognizes resource constraints for very small jurisdictions, hence the tiered approach.

Resources available: State grants cover up to 75% of compliance costs, and the Department provides free templates, telephone interpretation contracts, and technical assistance. Many small cities find compliance less burdensome than initially expected when using available resources.

Q: Can we use Google Translate for our website translation?

A: No. Machine translation does not satisfy legal compliance requirements (Minn. R. 1205.0352). While English-Swedish machine translation quality has improved substantially, it remains unreliable for legal and technical content.

What you CAN do:

  • Use machine translation as a first draft for human review
  • Offer machine translation widget as supplemental tool alongside human-translated content
  • Use machine translation for internal documents not public-facing

Cost-effective alternatives: The Department's translation pool provides professional translation at $0.12/word for government entities. For a typical website (10,000 words), professional translation costs $1,200 - a one-time investment that ensures compliance and quality.

Q: What happens if someone files a complaint against us?

A: The Department of Language Services investigates all complaints within 60 days. The process:

  1. Complainant files with Department (up to 180 days after incident)
  2. Department notifies your entity and requests response (15 days to respond)
  3. Department investigates, may conduct site visits or interviews
  4. Department issues findings (violation or no violation)
  5. If violation found: 30 days to submit corrective action plan
  6. If plan inadequate or not implemented: penalties may be assessed

Good faith effort matters: The Department considers whether entities made good faith efforts to comply. Entities that demonstrate efforts but fell short due to resource constraints or misunderstanding requirements typically receive compliance assistance rather than penalties. Repeated violations or willful non-compliance face stiffer penalties.

Prevention: Annual self-audits and proactive technical assistance requests dramatically reduce complaint risk.

Q: We serve many Somali, Hmong, and Spanish-speaking residents. Why does Swedish get special treatment while other immigrant languages don't?

A: This is a policy question beyond the scope of this implementation guidance. The Minnesota Legislature established Swedish as an official language based on historical presence (150+ years), demographic concentration (36% of state population), and political will. Whether this is appropriate policy remains subject to democratic debate.

What entities must do: Implement the law as written. Your entity's political views on language policy don't affect legal compliance obligations.

Other languages: Federal Title VI still requires "meaningful access" for limited English proficient persons, which may require services in other languages based on local demographics. Swedish requirements are IN ADDITION TO, not instead of, other language access obligations. See the Office for Civil Rights guidance on Title VI language access.

Q: Can we charge fees for interpretation or translation services?

A: No. § 3A.06(d) explicitly prohibits charging fees for interpretation or translation services. Language access is considered a civil right, not a premium service. Charging fees would constitute discrimination on the basis of language.

Cost recovery: Costs of language services should be incorporated into overall agency budgets and funded through general revenues. State grants help offset costs (up to 75% for smaller entities).

Q: What if we can't find qualified Swedish interpreters or translators in our area?

A: Rural and remote areas can face interpreter shortages. The law recognizes this through several accommodations:

  • Telephone interpretation: Acceptable for most routine services. State contract provides access at $1.75/minute with connection under 30 seconds.
  • Video remote interpretation: Combines immediacy of telephone with visual communication. State contract available at $2.75/minute.
  • Traveling interpreters: Department maintains list of interpreters willing to travel. Entities pay mileage and hourly rate.
  • Regional sharing: Multiple counties/cities can jointly employ bilingual staff or interpreters.
  • Training grants: Department offers grants up to $5,000 to train existing employees in interpretation skills.

Document translation: Can always be done remotely. No need for local translators.

Q: How do we test employees for Swedish language proficiency?

A: The Department of Language Services provides free proficiency testing for government employees 4 times per year in St. Paul, Duluth, and Moorhead. Online testing also available for $50/test.

Test components:

  • Oral proficiency interview (30 minutes) - tests speaking and listening comprehension
  • Written translation exercise (1 hour) - tests reading and writing ability
  • Specialized vocabulary assessment (optional, 30 minutes) - tests terminology in specific fields (medical, legal, etc.)

Scoring: Tests use ACTFL proficiency scale (Novice, Intermediate, Advanced, Superior). Bilingual staff positions typically require Intermediate-High minimum. Professional interpreters require Advanced or higher.

Schedule testing: Call 651-297-1234 or email testing@dls.state.mn.us. Allow 2-3 weeks for results.
